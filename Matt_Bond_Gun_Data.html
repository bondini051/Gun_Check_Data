Introduction¶

The Gun Data from the FBI's NICS (National Instant Criminal Background Check System) shows the gun permit checks from November 1998 to present. The data is broken into monthly reports and is available online through the FBI's website, FBI.gov. The data does not represent gun purchases but rather background checks. As the FBI's site advises, there is not a one to one correlation between checks and gun purchases. Each column in the data show different types of checks that occurr.

The question that I would like to answer is whether or not gun checks increase in the run up to the 2016 US Presidential Election. I will further break this down to see if the increases happen on party lines by showing the differences in permit checks between Red (Republican) and Blue (Democrat) States.

Data Cleaning: What needs to go?¶

In looking through the different columns and after checking the duplicates and null values we will replace null values with 0. I will create masks for my analysis for state, month and totals in order to get the answers I need. As I said previously, the FBI's description for the totals column makes it the most accurate for measuring the number of permits per state per month. Per the FBI website, they explain that each state has different rubics for checking gun permits. So the largest basket to catch all background checks will be the totals column.

I did not need much cleaning done, however, I will organize and mask certain protions of the data to work through in my analysis below. The question I would like to answer is whether or not gun checks increase during presidential election cycles. I will order the dates as 10 months prior to the election and 10 months post election in order to see if there are any significant changes in checks. The 5 US territories cannot vote in the election and so I will take those out of the data.

Conclusions¶

While in both 2012 and 2016 there are increases in background checks through the NICS, there is not a direct correlation between it being a run up to the election and the increase in checks. When one looks at the line chart showing the totals over the almost 20 year period of the NICS, every year there is an increase toward the end of the year and the a drop off at the beginning of the next year. There could be many reasons for this increase: retailers do better toward the end of the year and Christmas. "Black Friday" sales in November could be a reason for increases as could Christmas sales. There is not enough information to make a definate statement that political elections cause massive increases in background checks.

The data is incomplete by the FBI's own recollection. As this database started in the 1990's there were growing pains and therefore not all checks could have been recorded. Background checks do not mean a gun was purchased and furthermore more than one gun can be purchased per check so there is not a correlation of gun purchases to checks. Also, as we look at state to state numbers, a citizen of one state can drive into another state and get checked for a purchase or permit.

The one conclusion that I think is very evident is that at least since 2012 "Red States" have a far greater number of checks when compared to "Blue States". Red States had more than twice as many checks as Blue States. Red States had 85,928,763 total checks since 2012 while Blue States only had 37,851,622.
